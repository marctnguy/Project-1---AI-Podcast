Welcome back. Today we’re diving into one of those films that feels less like something you watch and more like something that happens to you: Gaspar Noé’s Climax. If you know Noé, you know the deal already, in a sense. Irreversible, Enter the Void, Love… he has a talent for turning cinema into a physical ordeal. But Climax might be one of his most intoxicating experiments yet.
The premise is deceptively simple. A dance troupe gathers to celebrate the end of rehearsals in the snowbound French countryside, drinks a sangria that’s been spiked with LSD, and slowly descends into panic, aggression, and delirium. But what’s fascinating is that Noé didn’t approach it like a tightly locked machine. He approached it like a live event, something to be discovered in the moment.
And that’s where the film becomes really interesting for cinephiles, because it’s built on paradox. Noé says he drew inspiration from disaster films and collapse narratives like The Towering Inferno, The Poseidon Adventure, even Shivers. Stories about people coming together to build something, only for everything to unravel. That’s Climax in a nutshell: collective energy curdling into catastrophe.
What makes it hit so hard is the dance. Noé is clearly obsessed with performers who can move beyond technique and into something almost trance-like. He speaks about watching dancers the way other people watch sport, with that same awe and fixation. And you can feel that in the film. Sofia Boutella, who had already retired from dance before stepping back into it here, leads a cast that aren’t really actors so much as bodies in motion, expressive, instinctive, alive.
And the production itself is almost as mad as the film. The shoot lasted just 15 days. The script, by all accounts, was barely more than a framework: beginning, middle, end, with almost no dialogue. The choreography was planned, but much of what happens around it was improvised. Noé would shoot in chronological order, then gather everyone around a monitor after each take, adjusting the story bit by bit, asking who wanted to kiss, who wanted to fight, who wanted to spiral further out of control. It’s a remarkably collaborative way to make a film that still feels so authored.
Boutella described the process as intense but liberating. Four hours of rehearsal a day, then take after take after take, sometimes as many as 17 for a single sequence. And because the film leans so heavily on movement rather than dialogue, the dancers had to communicate fear, hysteria, desire, and violence through their bodies alone. That’s why Climax feels so unusual. It’s not just chaotic — it’s choreographed chaos.
One sequence in particular sums up Noé’s method. The long, continuous shot in the second half, where paranoia spreads through the group and the mood flips from euphoric to monstrous, is a masterclass in controlled disorder. The sound had to be rebuilt in post because the production audio was such a glorious mess of screaming and music. But even that mess is the point. The film isn’t interested in polish. It wants sensation.
And maybe that’s why Climax feels gentler, in Noé’s own words, than some of his earlier work. Not less disturbing, certainly not less extreme, but strangely more playful, even funny in places. There’s something exhilarating about the sheer commitment of the dancers, something human beneath the horror. You want to be with them, to move with them, until the whole thing tips over.
So yes, Climax is another Gaspar Noé assault on the senses. But it’s also a celebration of bodies, rhythm, and collective energy — right up until that energy turns poisonous. And in that sense, it’s pure cinema: ecstatic, terrifying, and impossible to look away from.
Thanks for listening, and until next time, keep your sangria well away from the dance floor.
